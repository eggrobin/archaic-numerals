\section{Summary}
This document proposes Numeric\_Value property assignments for eight characters in the Cuneiform block.
It also proposes informative aliases, cross references, and informative notes,
as well as some adjustments to subheadings, for the character names
lists for the Cuneiform, Cuneiform Numbers and Punctuation, and Early Dynastic Cuneiform blocks.

\section{Rationale}
As noted in text proposed for the core specification in \cite[3]{L2/24-210}, several series
of cuneiform numerals are split between the Cuneiform Numbers and Punctuation block and the main
Cuneiform block, whereas other numeric characters have separately encoded unrelated lookalikes in the
Cuneiform block.

There are however no cross-references between these blocks, so that it can be difficult to locate
the appropriate character to use for a given number.
In some cases, the conventions used in ATF transliteration  of numeric notation \cite{inlineATF} 
do not match the character names; this can make it difficult to identify characters and locate their attestations.
For example, it recently took the author of this document and Steve Tinney\footnote{Who was a co-author of
the proposals \cites{L2/12-002}{L2/12-207R} to encode {\xsuxfont íë£} and {\xsuxfont íë§}!} a couple of days to figure out where
U+\textcsc{12463} {\xsuxfont íë£} \textcsc{CUNEIFORM SIGN ONE QUARTER GUR} and U+\textcsc{12464} {\xsuxfont íë§} \textcsc{CUNEIFORM SIGN ONE HALF GUR} came from,
initially suspecting they might be used in Ach√¶menid Persepolis;
they turned out to be from Ur III Umma, 700~km and 1500~years away,
see \cites{P429599}{P429605}[283\psq]{SigristGabbay2014},
but are described in digital corpora as numbers of bariga rather than fractions of a gur (of four bariga).

Further, the characters in the Cuneiform block, which rightfully have General\_Category=Other\_Letter on account of their non-numeric
usage, do not have Numeric\_Value assignments.
This is inconvenient when handling these numerals programmatically:
whereas \smash{\xsuxfont íêó} has Numeric\_Value=$3$ because it is $ÃÄ3\times\text{\xsuxfont íêï}$,
and \text{\xsuxfont íêï} accordingly has Numeric\_Value=$1$,
{\xsuxfont íêà} has Numeric\_Value=$3$ because it is $3\times\text{\xsuxfont íÅπ}$,
but \text{\xsuxfont íÅπ} has Numeric\_Value=NaN.
Note that there is precedent for assigning non-NaN Numeric\_Value
to characters with General\_Category=Other\_Letter; many such characters
are CJK unified ideographs, which get their Numeric\_Value from the Unihan database,
but eight are compatibility ideographs, which get their Numeric\_Value from UnicodeData.txt,
as is proposed here.

Some characters, such as U+\textcsc{121F9} \textcsc{CUNEIFORM SIGN LIMMU2}, are not merely lookalikes of numeric signs
(in this case U+\textcsc{12402} {\xsuxfont íêÇ} \textcsc{CUNEIFORM NUMERIC SIGN FOUR ASH}),
but are effectively duplicate encodings (limmu is the Sumerian word for ``four'').
No annotations nor changes to properties are proposed for those characters at the moment.
A separate proposal will be submitted at a later date to add them to DoNotEmit.txt and to add notes discouraging their use,
consistently with existing practice in the \cite{OSL}.

\section{Proposed changes to the Standard}
\label{proposal}
\subsection{Immediate character names list changes}
The following changes to NamesList.txt are proposed.
The affected characters are highlighted in blue in the charts appended to this document.
Note that these charts also incorporate the names list changes contingent on \cite{L2/24-210} below.
Cross references between unrelated lookalike characters are only proposed from non-numeric characters
to numeric characters, not in the other direction: since the Cuneiform block contains both numeric and
non-numeric signs, a user could be looking there for a numeric sign and pick the wrong character,
such as ZA {\xsuxfont íçù} for the number $4(\text{di≈°})=\text{\xsuxfont íêâ}$;
on the other hand, a user is unlikely to be looking in the Cuneiform Numbers and Punctation block for
the syllable /za/.
The annotations on the sign \text{\xsuxfont íÅπ} reflect its status as the first of two different numeral series:
the (di≈°) discrete counting numerals {\xsuxfont íÅπ}, {\xsuxfont íà´}, {\xsuxfont íêà}, {\xsuxfont íêâ}, {\xsuxfont íêä},
{\xsuxfont íêã}, {\xsuxfont íêå}, {\xsuxfont íêç}, {\xsuxfont íêç}, and the (barig) capacity measures {\xsuxfont íÅπ}, {\xsuxfont íëñ}, {\xsuxfont íëó}, {\xsuxfont íêâ}.
